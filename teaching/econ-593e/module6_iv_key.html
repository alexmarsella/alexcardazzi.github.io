Instrumental variables (IV) represents an important causal inference method in applied economists' tool box. IV can be difficult to implement because it requires a special variable -- an instrument -- to work. One cannot just proclaim a variable an instrument like a magician pulling a rabbit out of a hat. Understanding why a variable constitutes a good instrument is key to IV and typically requires deep understanding of the underlying mechanisms. Good instruments often originate outside the primary data set. This homework focuses on such a case.

The paper that forms the basis for this homework analyzes the tradeoffs and opportunity costs in the oceanic commercial fishing industry using data from around the world. Commercial fishing requires boats, and boats can be used to fish or for other purposes, including piracy. Boat owners will use their vessel for the activity that generates the highest return. This informs the research question addressed in the paper: do reduced fishing conditions lead boat owners to turn to piracy?

Fisheries represent a classic economic common pool resource that can be plagued by overuse (economists call this the "tragedy of the commons"). Because of this, various extra-governmental agencies monitor commercial fishing activity, generating good data on commercial fish production by country. International trade depends heavily on oceanic transport, and piracy represents an important threat to the free flow of goods across countries, leading to systematic data collection on acts of piracy.

Despite these excellent data sources, a simple OLS regression where the dependent variable captures acts of piracy and the explanatory variable reflects commercial fishing activity cannot tell us much about the causal effect of commercial fishing returns on piracy.
